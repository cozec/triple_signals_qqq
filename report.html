Is dollar-cost-averaging the Nasdaq really optimal?

A reproduction & stress-test of the three-signal (CAPE + Drawdown + VIX) dynamic-leverage framework from this X article. DCA $10,000 on the first trading day of every month, five start points → mid-2026. Leverage ETF = synthetic 3×-Nasdaq before 2010, real TQQQ after.

Step 3 — The three-signal framework

The idea: hold QQQ as the core, deploy TQQQ as a dynamically-sized accelerator — heavy only when the market is cheap, deeply fallen, and panicked; trimmed toward a floor when valuations are stretched.

The three base signals

① Valuation — CAPE percentile

  • below 20% → extremely cheap
  • above 70% → overvalued
  • above 85% → bubble-warning zone

② Trend & drawdown — DD

  • drawdown from the high > 20% → deeply oversold
  • a fall > 12% within 25 days → crash warning (used to cut leverage)

③ Panic — VIX

  • above 40 → extreme panic
  • below 12 → excessive calm (usually appears in overvalued zones)
All signals are lightly smoothed with a 5-day average, and evaluated only once — on the first trading day of each month.
In this reproduction, CAPE percentile uses a trailing 10-year window (the article doesn't specify one; a full-history percentile pins valuations permanently “expensive” after 2010). CAPE = S&P-500 Shiller CAPE as a market-valuation proxy.

“LOW” signals counted for bottoms: CAPE<20% + drawdown<−20% + VIX>40. Two or more firing ⇒ all-in TQQQ; one ⇒ tilt up; stretched/overheated ⇒ trim the TQQQ sleeve toward a 30% floor and stockpile cash (“ammo”) for the next bottom.

Reproduction vs the article's claims

The article's three-signal headline (34% IRR at only −52% drawdown, beating even buy-and-hold TQQQ on terminal value) was produced by an AI tool that iterated a dozen backtests on this exact 2000–2026 path — the classic signature of in-sample over-optimization. This reproduction deliberately does not fit to the drawdown target, so it shows the honest trade-off.

StrategyArticle claim (IRR / MDD / mult)This reproductionVerdict
QQQ DCA19.2% / -35.7% / 6.7×19.4% / -35.7% / 6.8×reproduced
TQQQ DCA37.3% / -83.7% / 58.7×37.7% / -83.7% / 59.9×reproduced
Three-Signal34.0% / -52.2% / 119.4×31.6% / -70.9% / 30.3×return only
The honest finding. You can capture most of TQQQ's return with the three-signal rules (~31.6% IRR) — but not at half the drawdown. The aggressive bottom-buying that drives the excess return necessarily rides the −90% collapses of 2000–02 and 2008, so the average drawdown (−70.9%) lands much closer to TQQQ than to the article's −52% claim.

Step 5 — Which of the three signals actually matters?

The framework is named for three signals, but do they pull equal weight? Measured two ways over the 318 monthly decisions from 2000 to 2026.

How often each fires

SignalMonthsFire rateWhat it's really detecting
DD — drawdown >20% off peak17655.3%a slow regime flag, not a crash timer (see note)
CAPE — percentile <20%4213.2%the binding gate on every buy decision
VIX — >4082.5%rare capitulation confirmation only
25-day crash < −12%144.4%the useful drawdown variant — fresh crashes
Why DD fires 55% of the time: QQQ did not reclaim its March-2000 peak until ~2015, so “drawdown >20% from the all-time high” was continuously true for most of 2002–2013. That makes from-peak DD a sticky “still underwater” flag rather than a sharp timing signal — the 25-day fast-drop (4% of months) is the real crash detector.

Crucially, all 42 “major bottom” (all-in TQQQ) months are exactly the CAPE-cheap months — because DD is almost always on, CAPE is the differentiator that gates the buy. VIX never triggered a major bottom on its own. The three signals are highly redundant: cheap valuation, deep drawdown and high VIX all coincide at the same events (crashes), so “2 of 3” mostly reduces to “CAPE-cheap + already-deep.”

Ablation — remove one signal at a time

The verdict. Removing any single signal moves average IRR by ≤1 point. The returns come overwhelmingly from the always-on TQQQ core weight, not the signal timing — the signals mainly nudge when you concentrate (a few points of drawdown), not the headline return. Of the three, CAPE carries the only real decision information (it gates both buying and trimming); from-peak DD is too sticky to time anything; VIX is largely redundant. The “three-signal” trio adds only marginally over simply holding a high-TQQQ core — consistent with the earlier finding that the edge is mostly in-sample story-telling.

Recent signal events — two case studies

What do the signals actually look like in the recent past? Two zoom-ins make the mechanics concrete. Panels show QQQ price, CAPE percentile, drawdown (and 25-day return), and VIX, each with its decision thresholds drawn in.

A) 2022–23 bear → the last buy trigger

The 2022 bear drove QQQ's drawdown below the −20% "deep" line and held it there for most of 2022 into 2023 — that single LOW signal kept the framework in minor-bottom mode (tilt up toward 75% TQQQ). But it never escalated to a major bottom: CAPE percentile only fell to ~0.45 (never <20% "cheap") and VIX peaked around 33 (never >40 "panic"), so only 1 of 3 signals was ever on. The green line marks 2023-05, the last month the smoothed drawdown was still below −20% before the recovery lifted it back up — the most recent signal-driven buy in the entire 2000–2026 sample.

B) March–April 2026 correction

A brisk ~−16% correction (raw peak-to-trough; ~−9% on the 5-day-smoothed drawdown the strategy reads) bottomed on 2026-03-30, with VIX rising only to ~28–31 — elevated but short of the 40 panic line — and CAPE nowhere near cheap. So zero LOW signals fired: no bottom, no TQQQ all-in. The only effect was that the dip knocked the index off its high, which switched off the "overvalued near-high → don't chase" de-risk for March–April and let the framework resume normal buying into the dip. A fast V-shaped rebound (the 25-day return swung to +19% by May) carried QQQ to new highs, and with CAPE pinned back at its ceiling the strategy returned to the don't-chase state. A textbook "shallow, fast" pullback — exactly the kind the framework rides through without crisis-buying.

Highlights.
  • The marquee move — a major bottom (all-in TQQQ) — fired only 4 times in 26 years (42 months, dominated by the long 2008–2011 cluster). The last one was 2011 — none since.
  • Months ≠ times. DD-deep shows 176 months but only 5 episodes — once QQQ is underwater it stays there for years (a persistent state, not a frequent trigger). The 25-day crash is the opposite: 14 months across 11 episodes — the real “fresh crash” detector.
  • VIX panic is the rarest real trigger: 8 months / 3 episodes (2008, 2011, 2020).
  • By episode count, the defensive “no-chase” trim is the most frequent action (20 times) — and almost the only thing firing in recent years, with CAPE pinned at its ceiling.
Signals fire constantly on the defensive side, but the aggressive crisis-buy the whole strategy is built around is extraordinarily rare — 4 times in 26 years. That is exactly why most of the realized return comes from the always-on TQQQ core rather than the timing.
